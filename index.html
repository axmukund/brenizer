How to Capture a Brenizer Mosaic

What is the Brenizer Method?

Shoot a grid of overlapping photos with a wide-aperture lens (f/1.4–f/2.8) to create a single large-format image with an impossibly shallow depth of field.

Recommended Shooting Patterns

3×39 frames — quick, easy
4×416 frames — good balance
5×525 frames — highest quality

Shooting Checklist

  • Lock focus — use manual focus or AF-lock on your subject, then don't refocus.
  • Lock exposure — switch to manual exposure or AE-lock to keep brightness consistent.
  • Rotate from one spot — pivot around the lens nodal point. Do not step or walk.
  • Overlap 30–50% — each frame should share at least 30% with its neighbor.
  • Shoot quickly — minimise time between frames to avoid subject movement.
  • Include your subject — place the subject in one or two of the central frames.
  • Avoid moving objects — people walking, cars, etc. cause ghosting at seams.

Common Failure Causes

  • Insufficient overlap — the stitcher can't find matching features between frames.
  • Parallax from translation — stepping sideways causes misalignment, especially with near objects.
  • Refocusing between shots — different focal planes prevent feature matching.
  • Moving subjects — creates ghosting or doubled edges at seam boundaries.
  • Exposure change — auto-exposure shifts cause visible bands in the mosaic.
  • Featureless regions — plain sky or walls don't have enough detail to match.

Tips for Best Results

  • Use a 50mm f/1.4 or 85mm f/1.8 for dramatic bokeh.
  • Start from the subject frame and spiral outward.
  • Shoot in portrait orientation for more vertical coverage per frame.
  • In low light, use a higher ISO rather than longer exposure (to avoid motion blur).
